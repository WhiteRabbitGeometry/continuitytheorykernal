# Continuity Theory Taxonomy

## Purpose

This document defines the core vocabulary of Continuity Theory (also referred to as Integrity Persistence Theory).

The purpose of the taxonomy is not to provide immutable definitions, but to establish a common language for analyzing persistence, identity, stewardship, transformation, adaptation, collapse, and inheritance across scales.

The taxonomy itself is considered a continuity and remains subject to revision through corrective inheritance.

---

# Core Concepts

## Existence

The prerequisite condition for continuity.

A continuity cannot exist without existence, though existence may contain many continuities.

---

## Continuity

A sequence of states connected through constitutive inheritance.

A continuity persists through transformation while retaining sufficient organizational inheritance to remain traceable as the same continuity.

Examples:

* A person
* A language
* A civilization
* A species
* A company
* A story
* A scientific theory

---

## Constitutive Inheritance

The transmission of organizational structure such that successor states remain intelligible only through reference to prior states.

Constitutive inheritance is the mechanism through which continuity persists.

Without inheritance there is no continuity.

---

## Identity

The recognizable pattern that emerges from continuity.

Identity does not create continuity.

Continuity creates identity.

Identity is therefore treated as an emergent property rather than a primitive one.

---

## Integrity

The preservation of correspondence across continuity.

Integrity measures whether a continuity remains aligned with:

* reality
* function
* principle
* lineage
* purpose

A continuity may persist while losing integrity.

---

## Stewardship

The maintenance and preservation of inheritance pathways.

Stewardship may be performed by:

* individuals
* institutions
* ecosystems
* technologies
* distributed systems

Stewardship is not ownership.

It is responsibility for transmission.

---

## Traceability

The degree to which lineage can be reconstructed.

A continuity with strong traceability preserves:

* provenance
* causal history
* inheritance pathways

Traceability permits correction.

---

## Correspondence

Alignment between representation and reality.

Correspondence asks:

Does the continuity understand itself accurately?

A continuity may possess strong persistence while exhibiting weak correspondence.

---

## Corrective Inheritance

The ability of reality, observation, critique, feedback, or evidence to modify future states of a continuity.

Healthy continuities preserve corrective inheritance.

Closed continuities suppress it.

---

# Continuity Dynamics

## Transformation

Change occurring within a continuity.

Transformation may strengthen, weaken, fragment, restore, or terminate continuity.

---

## Adaptation

Transformation that preserves sufficient inheritance while improving correspondence or viability.

Adaptation is not mere change.

It is useful change.

---

## Drift

Gradual change through time.

Drift is neutral.

It becomes problematic when:

* traceability declines
* correspondence declines
* stewardship fails

Languages, cultures, species, and institutions all drift.

---

## Debt

Accumulated divergence between:

* representation and reality
* function and requirement
* stewardship and need
* inheritance and understanding

Debt may remain invisible for long periods.

Many failures are debt collection events.

---

## Selection

Differential persistence among competing continuities.

Selection occurs in:

* biology
* economics
* information systems
* institutions
* cultures

---

## Recursion

A continuity modeling, observing, or modifying itself.

Recursion enables:

* self-reference
* self-correction
* self-deception

---

## Continuity Ecology

The study of how continuities coexist, compete, cooperate, consume, inherit, fragment, and restore one another across scales.

No continuity exists in isolation.

---

# Continuity Conditions

## Resilience

Capacity to remain viable under perturbation.

---

## Propagation

Capacity to reproduce or transmit into future states.

---

## Context Integrity

The preservation of meaningful relationships between local and larger continuities.

---

## Correction Capacity

The degree to which a continuity can recognize and respond to divergence.

---

# Outcomes

## Persistence

The continuity remains viable through transformation.

---

## Restoration

Integrity or correspondence is recovered after divergence.

---

## Fragmentation

A continuity separates into multiple descendant continuities.

Fragmentation is not inherently pathological.

---

## Speciation

Fragmentation that produces viable successor continuities.

---

## Absorption

A continuity loses independence but survives within another continuity.

---

## Collapse

Viability fails after accumulated debt, disruption, or ecological failure.

---

## Termination

Constitutive inheritance ceases.

The continuity no longer propagates.

---

## Simulacrum

A representation persists while constitutive inheritance weakens or disappears.

The appearance of continuity survives after continuity itself has degraded.

---

# Failure Modes

## Correspondence Failure

Representations diverge from reality.

---

## Traceability Failure

Lineage becomes difficult or impossible to reconstruct.

---

## Context Failure

Local continuity disconnects from larger continuity structures.

---

## Neglect

Necessary stewardship ceases.

---

## Dependency Failure

The continuity depends excessively upon a single steward, component, or pathway.

---

## Transmission Failure

Inheritance cannot be transferred.

---

## Propagation Failure

The continuity cannot reproduce into future states.

---

## Selection Failure

Inferior inheritances are preferentially transmitted.

---

## Simulacral Drift

Representation replaces inheritance.

The continuity becomes a copy of itself.

---

## Identity Lock

A continuity refuses adaptation in order to preserve represented identity.

---

## Continuity Overload

Accumulated inheritance exceeds adaptive capacity.

Examples:

* Bureaucratic accretion
* Technical debt
* Excessive legal complexity
* Legacy systems

---

## Continuity Drift

Gradual divergence from prior states.

Not inherently pathological.

Requires evaluation of traceability and correspondence.

---

## False Restoration

Attempting to restore an imagined past rather than the actual continuity lineage.

Examples:

* Nostalgic political movements
* Historical revisionism
* Personal identity idealization

---

## Predation

A continuity survives by extracting inheritance capacity from another continuity.

---

## Continuity Cancer

A continuity thrives locally while damaging the larger continuity that sustains it.

---

## Feedback Amplification

Errors reproduce and strengthen through recursive loops.

---

## Recursive Delusion

A model replaces reality.

Correction becomes interpreted as threat.

---

## Recursive Closure

Corrective inheritance becomes blocked.

The continuity becomes increasingly unable to learn from reality.

---

## Deferred Stewardship

Necessary action is postponed while debt accumulates.

---

## Habitat Loss

Supporting continuities disappear.

---

## Ecological Cascade

Failure propagates through dependent continuities.

---

## Severance

Lineage becomes broken, hidden, or unrecoverable.

---

# Guiding Questions

Every continuity analysis should begin with:

1. What continuity is being examined?
2. What persists through transformation?
3. What is actually inherited?
4. What is represented as inherited?
5. What lineage is traceable?
6. Where is integrity strong?
7. Where is integrity weak?
8. Where is debt accumulating?
9. What stewardship preserves transmission?
10. Where can corrective inheritance enter?
11. Failure of what continuity?
12. At what scale?

---

# Meta-Principle

The framework itself is a continuity.

Therefore it is subject to:

* drift
* debt
* adaptation
* fragmentation
* restoration
* collapse
* replacement

A continuity theory that cannot accept corrective inheritance is vulnerable to recursive closure.

The framework must therefore remain corrigible.
